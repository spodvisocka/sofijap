Harm Reduction School

Three years, 20 courses. In my time at Mainline Foundation I had the privilege of being able to develop the Harm Reduction School. This is a knowledge base of harm reduction work now available for free to the public, which you can access here.

My contribution included the research and writing for several of the courses:

  • Sister Spaces: Women Who Use Drugs
  • Mental Health & Stimulant Use
  • Invertentions for People Who Use Methamphetamine
  • Harm Reduction and Social Justice

I also built the web framework for the school, which included the design and implementation of the website, as well as the creation of a visual identity for the school. I created all of the graphics and interactive activities for the courses, and documented all of the training formats for future use.

This included interactive materials and graphics made with Adobe Illustrator and Javascript...

...infographics and data visualizations...

...responsive learning materials...

...and a combination of skills I learned on the job that allowed for engaging, multimedia learning experiences based on the needs of our stakeholders.

Finally, I oversaw the translation process for the courses, ensuring that the content was accessible to a wider audience. I worked with partner organizations and stakeholders to ensure that the content was culturally appropriate and linguistically accurate, managing the localization process into Russian, Ukrainian, Kiswahili, Myanmar, Tamil, Sinhala, Portuguese, Dutch, and Spanish.

XTC Shop

As a side project for Poppi Drugsmuseum, I led the development of a gamified research survey and helped set up and promote this as part of the XTC Shop exhibition. This included set-up at Westerpark and the Amsterdam Dealing with Drugs conference in 2024.

My contribution included the design of the survey, its implementation in SurveyJS, illustration, testing phases, physical exhibition set up, and the contracting of a back-end developer to help connect the project and deploy it to an Azure server.

Some Body

In 2022 I began working for the VU Art Science Gallery as gallery host, exhibition intern, and content designer. I also posted on the gallery's social media channels to promote the exhibition and engage with both the student community and outside visitors.

I attended the Rietveld Graduation Show to search for promising talent to display in the Some Body exhibition and designed the theme alongside the gallery team. I then interviewed the artists included in the exhibition and created a booklet in InDesign using archival material, the artists' work, and supporting documentation.

Finally, I contributed to the curation and installation of the exhibition, collaborating with art handlers and artists alike to ensure that the artworks were displayed effectively and that the visitor experience was engaging and informative.

The catalog for the exhibition was published by the gallery and distributed to attendees and supporters. We quickly had to move from the original 200 copies printed, and approximately 600 were distributed during the duration of the exhibition. A digital copy is available on the VU Art Science Gallery website here.

Drugs Monologen

My journey at Mainline Foundation began with Drugs Monologen, which was still known as Tales from the Drug Closet when I began working on it as a research assistant in 2022. My initial contributions included conducting literature reviews, organizing data, and assisting with the development of research protocols. As time went on and as my assistanship ended, I moved on to a more promotional and creative role in the project, working on flyers and posters for its launch at the original XTC Shop in Utrecht. This marked my first contact with Adobe, where I worked in Illustrator to start making these materials.

In 2025, I temporarily came back to the project to help set up an exhibition in Bogota, Colombia, for the International Harm Reduction Conference. The visual identity had changed, and so had the name, but the concept remained the same. I created new promotional materials for the exhibition and managed the logistics of its setup.

Finally, I went to Bogota to install the exhibition. Since we were on a tight budget, I had to be creative with the materials and resources available. Instead of booking the booths available at the conference, which were out of our range alone, I had to find alternative solutions. I opted to run the project in a tent on the exhibition floor, creating a unique and engaging space for visitors who wanted to record their stories.

While the experience was challenging, it was also incredibly rewarding to see the project come to life and to engage with visitors who shared their stories. I acquired about eight new contributions to the project over that weekend, each entailing a 15-45 minute oral history of their experiences, and marking a nearly 300% increase in the project's reach, which had acquired 3 contributions in the 3 months before.
